Overview: Projects should address a research question suitable for workshop submission
(NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR workshops). Deliverables include a written report (4-6 pages,
NeurIPS format), reproducible code repository, and a 15-minute final presentation.
Choose one option below or propose your own (Option 7, requires instructor approval
by Week 4).

Philosophy and Evaluation Criteria

This course project is designed to mirror the first stage of the research cycle. Given
the compressed timeline of a single semester, we prioritize intellectual honesty and
depth of analysis over raw performance metrics.

- The Value of Negative Results: In research, a "negative result" (e.g., "We
  implemented Successor Features for GFlowNets and found they do not converge on
  non-linear rewards") is a valuable contribution. If your hypothesis is disproven,
  your report should focus on a rigorous post-mortem: identify the mathematical or
  empirical bottlenecks that caused the failure.
- Scope and "Suggested" Tasks: The tasks listed under each option are suggestions to
  help you define a research direction. You are not required to complete every bullet
  point. You are encouraged to "zoom in" on a specific sub-question that interests you.
- Proof-of-Concept (PoC): You are not expected to beat state-of-the-art results on
  massive benchmarks. Demonstrating an effect or testing a property on a simplified,
  controlled environment (e.g., Hypergrid, CartPole, or a custom Minigrid setup) is
  often the best way to show research competence.
- Effort and Rigor: Your grade will be determined by the quality of your experimental
  design, the clarity of your reasoning, and your ability to relate your findings to
  the existing literature. Significant effort in implementation and critical thinking
  is highly rewarded, regardless of the final "success" of the agent.

PROJECT TASK DESCRIPTION

GFlowNet-Guided Bayesian Optimization

Bayesian Optimization (BO) is a powerful framework for optimizing expensive black-box
functions by carefully balancing exploration and exploitation (Snoek et al., NeurIPS
2012). The standard approach fits a surrogate model (typically a Gaussian Process) to
observed data and uses an acquisition function (Expected Improvement, Upper Confidence
Bound) to select the next query point. While GPs provide well-calibrated uncertainty
estimates, they scale poorly to high dimensions and struggle with complex, structured
input spaces.

Neural network surrogates offer flexibility and scalability but present a fundamental
challenge: how to quantify uncertainty for principled exploration? MC-Dropout
(Gal & Ghahramani, ICML 2016) provides a practical approximation by interpreting
dropout as approximate Bayesian inference: sampling random dropout masks yields an
implicit ensemble. However, this exploration is undirected: masks are sampled
uniformly at random, bearing no relationship to which perturbations would reveal
informative regions of the search space.

This project proposes GFN-BO: replacing random mask sampling with a learned
generative policy. A GFlowNet is trained to generate dropout masks with probability
proportional to their "improvement potential" - how much querying the resulting
maximizer would improve over the current best. This transforms exploration from
random perturbation to targeted search over the space of surrogate interpretations.

Formally, for mask m, define the masked surrogate's maximizer x_m = argmax_x f_e(x; m)
and reward:

R(m) = max(oracle(x_m) - y_best, 0)

The GFlowNet learns P(m) proportional to R(m)^beta, concentrating probability on
masks that lead to high improvement while maintaining diversity over promising
directions.

Research Questions

1. Can learned mask generation outperform random MC-Dropout for BO exploration? On
which problem classes (dimensionality, multimodality, noise level) are gains most
pronounced?
2. How should the GFlowNet handle non-stationarity as the surrogate evolves? Explore
contextual conditioning on dataset statistics, continual fine-tuning, and periodic
retraining.
3. What is the computational trade-off? GFlowNet training requires many surrogate
evaluations - when is this overhead justified by reduced oracle queries?
4. How does GFN-BO compare to other uncertainty-aware methods like Deep Ensembles and
concrete dropout?

Suggested Approach

Implement a sequential GFlowNet that generates dropout masks layer-by-layer for an
MLP surrogate. Address non-stationarity by conditioning the GFlowNet on an embedding
of the current dataset c = embed(D_t). Since oracle queries are expensive, use
surrogate predictions under held-out masks as proxy rewards during GFlowNet training.
Evaluate on standard benchmarks: Branin (2D), Hartmann-6 (6D), Ackley (10D), and
higher-dimensional synthetic functions. Report regret curves against number of oracle
queries. Prior work on GFlowNets for Dropout (Liu et al., ICML 2023) provides
relevant architectural insights.
